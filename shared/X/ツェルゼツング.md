# English translation
An explanation of the specific details regarding the ‘gaze’-based harassment operations carried out in the former East German city of Zittau, which constitute a historical fact (Google Gemini) Specific details and operational content regarding the ‘gaze’ and ‘staged surveillance’ in the Zersetzung programme are meticulously documented in internal Stasi (Ministry for State Security) documents disclosed after the collapse of the former East Germany, in ‘Directive 1/76 (Richtlinie 1/76)’, and in the testimonies of surviving victims.

The specific methods by which they systematically dismantled the psyche of their targets using ‘eyes’ and ‘presence’ were based on the following system:
1. The principle of ‘demonstrative observation’ as seen in internal documents  
   The Stasi’s basic policy was to deliberately alternate between ‘covert surveillance’—monitoring in secret—and ‘demonstrative observation’ (Demonstrative Beobachtung)—deliberately making the subject aware that they were being watched.
     - Symbolising the message ‘I am watching you’  
       When a target was walking, several operatives would pass them by, stopping completely for just a moment to stare intently at the target with expressionless faces. They deliberately staged this ‘unnatural stare’, which was distinct from a mere meeting of eyes.
     - The relay of gazes (the illusion of a cordon)  
       On station platforms or in the street, a person identified as ‘A’ would stare intently at the target; when the target moved on, a person identified as ‘B’ would take over, continuing the stare. This instilled in the target a powerful sense of paranoia—the feeling that ‘the entire city, the whole of society, is watching me’.
   
2. Specific Descriptions in Everyday Spaces (Victim Testimonies and Records)  
   Decrypted documents and victims’ diaries describe elaborate schemes that go far beyond the mere act of ‘staring’.
   - ‘Theatrical’ Deployment Along Commuting and Travel Routes
     - Deployment at crossroads and ambush points: At the corners of the route the target takes every morning, a person wearing the same coat, or a different person each day, would invariably be standing there, turning their body to follow the target with their gaze until they had passed.
     - Encirclement on public transport: When the target boards a train or bus, operatives (or unofficial collaborators: IM) sit in a circle around them, pretending to read a newspaper whilst continuously sending glances through the gaps in the pages or peering directly at their face. If the target returns their gaze, the operatives either look away as if nothing had happened or, conversely, stare back with an extremely cold expression.
   - The Destruction of the Sanctuary of the ‘Home’  
     The most insidious feature of Zersetzung is that it targets the ‘home’, which is an individual’s safest place.
     - Relentless Staring from Opposite Windows: From buildings opposite or from cars parked suspiciously, binoculars or camera lenses are deliberately aimed at the target’s room. Driven by the fear of being watched, the target begins to live with the curtains drawn even at home, rejecting the light.
     - Suggestion of a gaze through intrusion and ‘rearrangement’: Whilst the target is away, the perpetrator unlocks the door and enters, not to steal items, but to carry out subtle acts of manipulation (displaying traces) such as ‘turning the chair slightly towards the window’ or ‘changing the angle of the photo frame on the desk’. This plunges the target into a state of mental panic, convinced that ‘even whilst I was away, someone’s gaze was creeping around this room’.
3. The perpetrator’s exclusion of words as seen in the accounts  
   What is strictly adhered to in the specific records of these acts is the rule that ‘under no circumstances are words spoken directly to the target (they are not addressed)’.

   Why are words not used?  
   If they were to threaten the target verbally by saying, ‘I’m watching you’, this would be easily reported to third parties as extortion or a clear cause for concern, and could be proven as such. However, if they merely ‘stand there and stare’ or ‘look away unnaturally’, no legal evidence remains whatsoever.

   Precisely because no words are used, the target continues to ruminate on ‘why this is happening to me’ in their mind, becoming trapped in a hell of self-questioning. The Stasi’s psychologists had fully incorporated the principle that “humans break down most rapidly when subjected to a relentless, inexplicable stare” into their operational practice.
4. Division of labour as systematic harassment  
   ​What is clear from these accounts is that this was not merely individual harassment, but rather “a manualised, shift-based operation”.

   Stasi records show that for a single target, several or sometimes dozens of collaborators were deployed at staggered intervals. Those tasked with casting glances on the victim’s morning commute, those assigned to stare from the adjacent table in a café at lunchtime, and those who prowled around the victim’s home at night—the perpetrators handled these duties dispassionately as ‘tasks within working hours’.

   It is precisely this gap between the perpetrators’ overwhelming systematic approach and the victims’ complete isolation’ that explains why Zersetzung’s account is still studied today as the worst possible example of psychological violence and control.

# Copilot on whether it is malicious

I asked Copilot a simple question.

> We can safely say that this is malicious?

Yes — Zersetzung itself was absolutely malicious.  

Not “possibly”, not “arguably”, but deliberately, structurally, and systematically malicious in its intent and execution.

Let me be precise and analytical, because you appreciate clarity and distinctions.

🎯 Core point

Zersetzung was state‑engineered psychological destruction.

Its purpose was to break a person’s stability, credibility, relationships, and sense of reality without leaving evidence.

That is the definition of malicious.

🧩 Why Zersetzung qualifies as malicious (in a strict, analytical sense)

1. Intent to harm

The Stasi’s own Directive 1/76 explicitly states that the goal was to:

“decompose, paralyse, disorganise, and isolate”

(Richtlinie 1/76, Stasi archives)

This is not surveillance for security.

It is harm as policy.

2. Systematic psychological violence

The programme used:

* demonstrative observation
* staged surveillance
* home intrusion and subtle manipulation
* social isolation
* professional sabotage
* rumour campaigns
* gaslighting through environmental cues

These are not neutral actions. They are designed to destabilise the mind.

3. Bureaucratic organisation of cruelty

Zersetzung was not a rogue operation. It was:

* manualised
* shift‑based
* documented
* evaluated
* refined by psychologists

Malice becomes institutional when it is routinised.

4. Targeting civilians, not criminals

Most victims were:

* dissidents
* artists
* students
* church members
* people who criticised the regime
* people who simply knew the wrong person

This is persecution, not policing.

5. Deliberate avoidance of evidence

The Stasi intentionally avoided physical violence because:

* bruises can be photographed
* threats can be recorded
* injuries can be reported

So they engineered invisible violence — the most insidious form.
